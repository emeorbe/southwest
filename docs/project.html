The politeness system and the emergence of a Sprachbund

The great enigma of Spanish and Portuguese is the enormous complexity in the system of pronouns of address throughout their geographical areas. Despite the amount of studies that have analysed the usage of the different alternatives, it is still unknown why this feature has prompted three main regions that are not even individually homogeneous. The Atlantic Ocean emerges as the boundary that splits the American and the European realities, but the south-western area of the Iberian Peninsula shows a mixed system that combines characteristics of both of them. The literature agrees in pinpointing a conjoint evolution in the Hispanic and Lusophone world, but everything changes in the 1700’s.

The War of Spanish Succession (1700-1714) resulted in the overthrow of the Habsburgs and the new reign of the Bourbons. Their establishment in the Hispanic world marked the beginning of France as the centre of prestige for the entire country as well as the gradual decay of the southwest as a hub between America and Europe. By the early 19th century, the American possessions declared independence and Madrid became the focus of prestige within Spain. The same applies to Portugal, which lost Brazil, fostered Lisbon as the epicentre of prestige and encouraged the colonisation of Africa.

The late 18th century and the early 19th century attested profound changes in society as a result of two very important events: the Industrial Revolution and the French Revolution. The former provoked the exodus from the countryside to the city and the emergence of the middle class; the latter changed the beliefs of western societies and the way people had to address each other. Politeness started to favour solidarity and to gradually dismiss hierarchy and deference. Simultaneously, western countries promoted the creation of standard languages, prompting a type of diglossia between standard and vernacular, urban and rural, literate and illiterate…

All these happenings materialised at the same time as America and the Iberian Peninsula followed different political paths. Nevertheless, the southwest maintained the secular relationships that had linked it to the other side of the Atlantic although it began to look at elsewhere within Iberia. The 1700’s and 1800’s witnessed the decline of the southwest after centuries of prestige, the reciprocal isolation between the Hispanic and Lusophone Atlantic shores, besides the creation of three macro-areas embodied in the system of forms of address. But, above all, the 18th and 19th centuries represented the time period in which the south-western Sprachbund ended up establishing itself as an own region that has exhibited both American and European linguistic features until the present being.

This project aims to find the geographical, sociological, political, pragmatic and grammatical patterns that favoured the emergence and later establishment of the south-western Sprachbund. In order to do so, I will research data that account for the evolution of the system of forms of address in the 18th and 19th centuries in western Andalusia and southern Portugal. My investigation will shed light on the likely influence of the other Ibero-Romance languages and the mutual conditioning of Portuguese and Spanish, but it will also determine whether the New World and the south-western Sprachbund have influenced one another despite the American independences or either of them has taken the lead over the other.

I invite you to dive into the complexity of politeness and forms of address, because they reflect much more than a simple strategy to treat someone: they hide the way a society thinks, they hint at geopolitical relationships, they reveal our beliefs.
